犬いぬの ぽち
inu no pochi
Pochi the Dog

これは 犬いぬ です。

kore wa inu desu.

This is a dog.

犬いぬの 名前なまえは ぽち です。

inu no namae wa pochi desu.

The dog's name is Pochi.

ぽちは りんごが 好すき です。

pochi wa ringo ga suki desu.

Pochi likes apples.

今日きょう、ぽちは りんごを 食たべました。

kyou, pochi wa ringo o tabemashita.

Today, Pochi ate an apple.

とても 美味おいしかった です。

totemo oishikatta desu.

It was very delicious.

Grammar Notes

Line 1: これは 犬 です。

This introduces the most basic Japanese sentence structure: A は B です (A is B).

• は (wa): This is the Topic Particle. Even though it's written with the hiragana 'ha', it is pronounced 'wa' when used as a particle. It tells the listener, "As for [Topic]...". Here, the topic is kore (this).
• です (desu): This works like "is/am/are" in English, placed at the very end of the sentence to make it polite.

Line 2: 犬の 名前は ぽち です。

• の (no): This is the Possessive Particle. It works exactly like an apostrophe-S ('s) in English. Inu (dog) + no + namae (name) = Dog's name. It links two nouns together.

Line 3: ぽちは りんごが 好き です。

• が (ga): This is the Subject Particle. When saying you "like" something in Japanese, you don't use the direct object particle. Instead, the thing you like is marked with ga.

• 好き (suki): This translates to "like", but grammatically in Japanese, it's actually an adjective, not a verb! Literally, the sentence is closer to "As for Pochi, apples are likable."

Line 4: 今日、ぽちは りんごを 食べました。

• を (o): This is the Direct Object Particle. It marks the thing that the verb is acting upon. What is being eaten? The apple (ringo). So, it becomes ringo o. (Note: It is written with the hiragana 'wo', but pronounced just 'o').

• 食べました (tabemashita): This is the past tense of the verb taberu (to eat). The -mashita ending makes it past tense and polite. Japanese verbs always go at the very end of the sentence!

Line 5: とても 美味しかった です。

• とても (totemo): An adverb meaning "very".

• 美味しかった (oishikatta): This is the past tense of the adjective oishii (delicious). In Japanese, adjectives conjugate (change shape) just like verbs do to show time! Dropping the final 'i' and adding 'katta' turns "is delicious" into "was delicious."
